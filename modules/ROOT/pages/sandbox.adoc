== Introduction

Data can be fully conceptualized as a set of  _statements_, each of which is an expressions of information about a (real-world or otherwise) _thing_. Many different languages and metamodels exist to make such statements, but to be able to state something about some _thing_, every single one of them relies on every _thing_ having an identifier to reference it by.

== Goals

For each definition created in the name of Netbeheer Nederland, the following IRIs MUST be devised:

. **Identifier** of the real-world object or notion that's reing defined on the web.
.

. Each IRI must resolve to:
** a formal, machine-readable representation of the meaning of the referent
** human-friendly documentation on the .
. Each IRI minted by Netbeheer Nederland MUST conform to the principles laid out in this document.

=== No punning

A resource MUST NOT be an instance of both `skos:Concept` and `owl:Class`.

Even though OWL 2 supports this practice of _punning_, this comes with the implication of needing to use the OWL Full profile which severely limits the reasoning capabilities:

"However, an IRI may denote different entity-types (e.g. both an individual and a class) at the same time. This possibility, called “punning,” has been introduced to allow for a certain amount of metamodeling[...] To allow a more readable syntax, and for other technical reasons, OWL 2 DL requires that a name is not used for more than one property type (object, datatype or annotation property) nor can an IRI denote both a class and a datatype.[...]"
-- Section 8.3 <<OWL2-PRIMER>>

Several strategies of combining SKOS and OWL are described in <<OWL-SKOS>>. That article also explains in more detail what the implications of punning are.

* If the agent is a machine, a formal and machine-readable description of the resource MUST be provided.

IMPORTANT: If the meaning of some thing changes, it needs a new URI. Therefore, model element URIs are tightly coupled to their ontology definitions, and changing metamodel implementations -- which entails we cannot guarantee meaning is preserved -- means a new URI is necessary too. Therefore, it really makes sense to tightly couple specific ontologies (for example an OWL ontology) to their URIs.




== Frequently asked questions

Wouldn't it be more proper to assign real-world abstract concepts (such as _person_) an identity URI, and have different kinds of models such as ontologies and thesauri express different perspectives (i.e. definitions)?::
    This seems like a great ambition, but there is a major issue with it, namely that identifying something is inherently coupled to the sense of that thing, i.e. the meaning is tightly coupled to the identifier. Assigning a URI to a real-world concept like _person_ seems straight-forward, but what is it you are referring to? With real-world objects that are concrete this could make sense, but with abstract concepts this is too vague. You need to define the sense of what you are referring to before you can refer to it. So, to simplify, the models you create and in which you express the sense/meaning of things, also provide the identifiers tightly coupled to them.

Why does a model like an ontology, thesaurus or schema need an identification URI? Aren't these information resources, i.e. isn't their existence inherently digital and on the web?::

Now suppose you are devising a formal definition of the data product and need to refer to the associated information model that belongs to it. It makes perfect sense to assign this model a URI, even though you may not have a representation yet. == Terms and definitions

[[def-resource]] resource:: The term _resource_ is used in a general sense for whatever might be identified by a URI. See: <<AWWW>>.

[[def-generic-resource]] generic resource:: A <<def-resource>> insufficiently specific to have only a single representation. See also: <<TBL-GENERIC>>.

[[def-resource-description]] resource description:: A machine-readable representation of the resource, typically in some serialization of RDF.

[[def-information-representation]] information resource:: A <<def-resource>> which has the property that all of its essential characteristics can be conveyed in a message. See also: <<AWWW>>.
